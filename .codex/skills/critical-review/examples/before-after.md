# Critical Review — worked example

A short worked example so you can see the shape of what the skill returns.

## Input (draft paragraph)

> *Australia's unemployment rate has risen sharply to 5.1% in early 2026, marking the third consecutive quarterly increase. This trend demands immediate intervention through expanded wage subsidies, which have proven effective in every developed economy that has tried them.*

## What critical-friend returned

> **The argument as I read it.** The claim is that Australia's labour market is deteriorating and that wage subsidies are the right response, justified by their universal success elsewhere.
>
> **Sharpest pushbacks:**
>
> 1. **"Every developed economy" is a sweeping generalisation.** Wage subsidies are contested in OECD policy literature — Germany's Kurzarbeit is often cited as successful, but US Paycheck Protection is more mixed. The argument needs the comparison narrowed and the success criteria specified.
> 2. **"Demands immediate intervention" smuggles in urgency.** Three quarters of rising unemployment from a historically low base may or may not constitute a crisis. The draft assumes a threshold without naming it.
> 3. **No counterfactual is considered.** What happens without intervention? Is the rise cyclical, structural, or post-stimulus correction? The intervention choice depends on the diagnosis.
>
> **Strongest counter-position.** A thoughtful Treasury sceptic would argue that wage subsidies are politically attractive but economically dubious: they prop up jobs that the market is signalling shouldn't exist, delay structural adjustment, and create fiscal commitments that are hard to unwind. The Kurzarbeit comparison is misleading because Germany's labour market institutions are fundamentally different from Australia's.
>
> **What would have to be true for the writer to be wrong:**
> - The unemployment rise must be transitory (not structural)
> - Wage subsidies must outperform direct income support on cost-per-job-saved
> - The Australian institutional context must be closer to Germany's than to the US's
>
> **Voices missing:** small business owners (who bear administrative costs), Treasury (fiscal sustainability), affected workers themselves (do they want subsidies or transitions?), the Reserve Bank (monetary policy interaction).

## What fact-checker returned

> **Claims checked: 2**
>
> **✗ Incorrect (1)**
> - **Claim:** "Australia's unemployment rate has risen sharply to 5.1% in early 2026"
>   **Actual figure:** 4.2% as of March 2026
>   **Source:** Australian Bureau of Statistics, Labour Force Survey, March 2026 (abs.gov.au)
>   **Suggested correction:** "Australia's unemployment rate stands at 4.2% as of March 2026, up from 3.9% in late 2025."
>
> **? Unverifiable (1)**
> - **Claim:** "Wage subsidies have proven effective in every developed economy that has tried them"
>   **Why unverifiable:** Sweeping comparative claim with no specific dataset to check against. OECD literature is mixed.
>   **Suggestion:** Replace with named programmes and named outcomes (e.g. "Germany's Kurzarbeit reduced unemployment volatility by X% during 2008–2010 per OECD analysis…").

## Synthesised summary the skill produced

> - **Strongest pushback:** "Every developed economy" is unsupportable; the comparison needs narrowing.
> - **Most significant factual issue:** Unemployment figure is wrong (5.1% claimed; actual is 4.2% per ABS).
> - **Recommendation:** Revise. The factual error is fixable in a sentence; the argument needs the comparison narrowed and the counterfactual addressed before it goes out.
